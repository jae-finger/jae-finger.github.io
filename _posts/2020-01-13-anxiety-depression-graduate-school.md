It’s sort of a secret on college campuses in America I was told.
Maybe not every college and not a complete secret, but from what the doctor told me, it is most graduate schools.
“It’s nothing to be ashamed of,’’ she said. “Probably half the students are on medication like this. If it’s not for depression then anxiety — or both.”
If my doctor was right, then every other graduate student has needed help for anxiety and depression. Could this be true? Is the person on my left but not my right is in a similar struggle?
I figured the doctor was exaggerating to console me. Because if so many students are struggling, why is no one talking about it?

**Turns out some people are asking why (like me)**
Every other year, Nature, the world’s leading science journal, conducts a survey of PhD students. Over 6,000 individuals responded to 2019’s questionnaire covering topics like: reason for going to graduate school, country of origin, satisfaction, and mental health.
Have half of these students reached out for help for anxiety and depression too? What if it’s more? Then what could be causing it? Is it only a certain group or subset? I wanted to explore two explanations for mental health struggles in graduate school: inadequate attention from our advisors and a monstrous workload.

**Maybe we don’t get enough attention**
I hard a tough decision when I had finally narrowed my choices down to two graduate programs. I joked that I had to choose between one lab — a family holding hands — and the other describing a “team” of independent and unrelated projects. Are students with a clingy advisor instead of one with a more laissez-faire attitude smothered? Or do some busy and distant advisors neglect their students?

It doesn’t look like advisor attention impacts the percentage of students seeking help. There is only a 6% difference in reaching out whether students got plenty or little attention from their advisor. We would expect there to be a benefit in having more advisor 1:1 time. This is visible with the percentage of students seeking help slightly decreasing with more 1:1 time. But there is not enough evidence to point to this hypothesis having much impact.

**Perhaps we work too hard**
Another influence on the graduate student psyche is workload. It’s not too hard to find a comic or meme not stereotyping a frumpy, overworked graduate student complete with a matching set of coffee cup and bags under their eyes.

![Grad School Energy Levels by XKCD](https://miro.medium.com/max/600/0*i3tD6512uVOxsMl-)

It’s a no-brainer then that an environment of constant work, never-ending deadlines, and a perpetual lack of sleep would drive a large portion of students to seek help. But do students with a higher workload seek help more? How about students with a lighter workload? It wouldn’t be hard pressed to assume they would have more time to relax, find time to eat, and catch up on sleep.

Similarly, it doesn’t look like workload impacts the percentage of students seeking help. There is only a 10% gap between students seeking help despite the wide variety in workload. We would expect the highest stress with the heaviest workload. Yet, there isn’t much difference between rates of students with the highest and lowest workloads. Keeping the workload to a traditional part- or full-time level seems to be the least stressful to students.

**Back to the drawing board**
Graduate school is complicated. It’s a in between-zone where the individual is a student and professional — both in title and work/life commitment. The unique situation of graduate school can amplify many different, low-level problems. Or perhaps the mounting pressure after almost two decades in school pries at any chink in your armor.

Now two years after leaving graduate school, I know that my armor had a crack in it. A hidden autoimmune disease was likely to blame for my mental health struggles in graduate school. Would I have experienced these struggles if I had opted out of the experience? Probably. But it still begs the question why so many students struggle mentally and emotionally during their graduate career.
